Selected Research Projects

Continuous flow-through iron electrocoagulation for selenate removal

Selenium (Se) is an essential trace element, but at elevated levels it is toxic to humans and aquatic life. We developed a continuous flow-through iron electrocoagulation process to remove selenate [Se(VI)] from water and bridge laboratory studies with real-world treatment applications. Under optimized conditions, this system achieved over 98% selenium removal with reactor residence times as short as 11 s. We further evaluated the process in complex waters containing naturally occurring salts and organic matter that commonly inhibit treatment performance. Advanced solid characterization revealed how reactive iron minerals capture and chemically transform selenium into stable, low-toxicity forms. Together, this work provides mechanistic guidance for designing modular, autonomous, and effective electrochemical treatment systems for selenium-contaminated waters.

Interfacial chemistry of critical elements

Rare earth elements and platinum group elements are critical materials used in clean energy technologies, electronics, and catalysis. Our work focuses on the interfacial chemistry of these critical elements at mineral and aqueous interfaces. We quantified their adsorption behavior under diverse environmental conditions. Using spectroscopy, we resolved how these elements interact with reactive mineral surfaces at the molecular scale and identified the fundamental binding mechanisms. We further developed predictive models to understand and forecast the occurrence of these critical elements across different environmental settings. These insights can inform extraction strategies and help identify new sources of critical elements.

Chemical and physical processes governing arsenic, chromium, and uranium removal in electrocoagulation

Arsenic, chromium, and uranium are toxic metals that are frequently present in groundwater and pose serious risks to human health. Our work investigates how these contaminants are removed under realistic water treatment conditions. We show that contaminant removal is controlled not only by chemical reactions such as reduction and adsorption, but also by physical processes, including particle aggregation and settling. By systematically varying water chemistry, we reveal how naturally occurring constituents can either promote or inhibit removal by altering both reaction pathways and particle behavior. We aim to establish a generalized mechanistic understanding that guides the design of robust and reliable electrochemical systems for safe drinking water treatment.
